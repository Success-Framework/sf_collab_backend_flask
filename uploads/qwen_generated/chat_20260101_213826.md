# Quantum Computing – A Beginner’s Guide

Quantum computing is a paradigm that leverages the principles of quantum mechanics to process information in ways that classical computers cannot. Below is a structured overview that covers the fundamentals, core concepts, key technologies, and practical implications.

---

## 1. What is Quantum Computing?

| Classical Computer | Quantum Computer |
|--------------------|------------------|
| Uses bits (0 or 1) | Uses qubits (superposition of 0 and 1) |
| Deterministic operations | Probabilistic / wave‑function evolution |
| Sequential logic gates | Parallel superposition & entanglement |
| Exponential memory for certain problems | Potential polynomial‑time solutions for specific tasks |

**Bottom line:** Quantum computers exploit quantum phenomena—superposition, interference, and entanglement—to perform certain computations far more efficiently than classical machines.

---

## 2. Core Quantum Principles

| Principle | Explanation | Relevance to Computing |
|-----------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Superposition** | A qubit can be in a linear combination of states: \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\). | Enables parallel evaluation of many inputs simultaneously. |
| **Entanglement** | Two or more qubits share a joint state; measurement of one instantly affects the others. | Provides correlations that classical systems cannot replicate; key for quantum algorithms. |
| **Interference** | Quantum amplitudes can add or cancel, amplifying correct outcomes while suppressing wrong ones. | Core to algorithmic speed‑ups (e.g., Grover, Shor). |
| **Quantum Decoherence** | Loss of quantum coherence due to environmental interactions. | Major technical challenge; requires error correction & isolation. |
